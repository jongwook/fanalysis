For any $$f \in C([0, 1])$$, we have shown that $$f \in L^1 ([0, 1])$$ in the previous problem, which implies the norm $$ \left \| ~f~ \right \|_{L^{1}}$$ is defined.
Furthermore,

  1. $$ \left \| ~f  \right \|_{L^{1}} = \int_0^1 | ~f~ | dx = 0 ~ \Leftrightarrow ~ f = 0$$
  2. $$ \left \| ~ \lambda f ~ \right \|_{L^{1}} = \int_0^1 | ~\lambda f~ | dx = \int_0^1 | ~\lambda~| |~ f~ | dx =  | ~\lambda~| \int_0^1 |~ f~ | dx = | ~\lambda~| \left \| ~f  \right \|_{L^{1}}$$
  3. $$ \left \| ~ f + g  \right \|_{L^{1}} = \int_0^1 | ~f + g ~ | dx \leq \int_0^1 | ~f~| + |~g~| dx = \int_0^1 | ~f~| dx + \int_0^1 |~g~| dx =  \left \| ~f  \right \|_{L^{1}}  +  \left \| ~g  \right \|_{L^{1}} $$ 

Therefore, $$ \left \| ~\cdot~ \right \|_{L^{1}}$$ is a norm on $$C([0, 1])$$.
