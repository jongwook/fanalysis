For $$x = (x_1, x_2), y = (y_1, y_2) \in \mathbb{R}^2$$ define $$a( x , y ) = x_1 y_1 + 2 x_2 y_2$$, then $$a$$ is a linear coercive bilinear form since

  • $$a(\lambda x, y) = a(x, \lambda y) = \lambda x_1 y_1 + 2 \lambda x_2 y_2 = \lambda a(x, y) $$
  • $$a(x + x' , y) = (x_1 + x'_1) y_1 + 2 (x_2 + x'_2) y_2 = a(x, y) + a(x', y) $$
  • $$a(x , y + y') = x_1 (y_1 + y'_1) + 2 x_2 (y_2 + y'_2) = a(x, y) + a(x, y') $$
  • $$a(x, x) = x_1^2 + 2x_2^2 \geq x_1^2 + x_2^2 = \| x \|$$
For $$y = (y_1, y_2) $$, $$ f(x) = ay_1 - by_2 $$ is in $$\mathbb{R}^*$$, and Lax-Milgram lemma implies that
  • $$x_1 y_1 + 2 x_2 y_2 = a y_1 - b y_2 $$ , or $$a(x, y) = f(y)$$ has a unique solution $$ x \in \mathbb{R}^2$$.
  • $$f(y)$$ is linear and continuous from $$\mathbb{R}^{2*}$$ to $$\mathbb{R}^2$$.
Since $$\mathbb{R}^{2*}$$ can be identified as a space of $$[a, b]$$, $$f$$ is the unique solution and a linear and continuous function of $$[a, b]$$.
