To show the uniqueness of the solution of $$f(x) = x$$, suppose that there are two different solution $$l_1, l_2$$, then

$$d(l_1, l_2) = d(f(l_1), f(l_2)) \leq K d(l_1, l_2) $$.

Since $$l_1 \neq l_2$$ implies $$d(l_1, l_2) \neq 0$$, we have $$K \geq 1$$ which contradicts that $$f$$ is a contradiction: $$K < 1$$.


For any $$m > n$$, using the triangle inequality gives:

$$
\begin{eqnarray}
d(x_n , x_m) & \leq & d(x_n, x_{n+1}) + d(x_{n+1}, x_{n+2}) + \cdots + d(x_{m-1}, x_m) \\
& \leq & K^n d(x_0, x_1) + K^{n+1} d(x_0, x_1) + \cdots + K^{m-1} d(x_0, x_1) \\
& = & (K^n + K^{n+1} + \cdots + K^{m-1}) d(x_0, x_1) \\
& \leq & (K^n + K^{n+1} + \cdots + K^{m-1} + K^m + \cdots) d(x_0, x_1) \\
& = & \frac {K^n} {1-K} d(x_0, x_1) \\
\end{eqnarray}
$$

For any $$\epsilon > 0$$, we can choose $$N$$ such that $$\frac {K^n} {1-K} d(x_0, x_1) < \epsilon$$, then for any $$m > n > N$$ we have

$$d(x_n , x_m) \leq \frac {K^n} {1-K} d(x_0, x_1) < \epsilon$$, therefore $$(x_n)$$ is a Cauchy sequence, and since $$X$$ is complete, $$(x_n)$$ converges to a point $$l \in X$$.


$$f$$ is continuous, since if $$K = 0$$ then $$f$$ is constant, and if $$K > 0$$ then for any $$\epsilon > 0$$ we can pick $$\delta = \epsilon / K$$ and

$$d(x, x') < \delta \; \Rightarrow \; d(f(x), f(x')) \leq K d(x, x') < K \delta = \epsilon$$, so $$f$$ is uniformly continuous, therefore continuous.


From $$f$$ being continuous we have $$x_n \rightarrow l \Rightarrow f(x_n) \rightarrow f(l)$$. Meanwhile $$f(x_n) = x_{n+1}$$ also converges $$l$$,

Therefore from the uniqueness of limits we have $$f(l) = l$$.


$$\therefore \;$$ There exists $$l \in X$$ such that $$(x_n)$$ converges to $$l$$ and $$l$$ is the unique solution to equation $$f(x) = x$$.
